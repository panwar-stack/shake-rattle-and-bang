I have spent much of the last decade building and leading growth engineering teams. I will do my best to share what I have learned, and I hope to learn from fellow growth practitioners.

# The Growth Engineering Maturity Curve
Most growth engineering teams move through a fairly predictable maturity curve. The stages are less about company size and more about how repeatable, measurable, and scalable the growth system has become.

## 1. Ad hoc growth

At this stage, growth work is reactive. Engineers help with landing pages, sign up flows, referral links, campaign support, tracking fixes, and one off experiments.

The team is usually asking

What can we ship quickly that might increase users, activation, or revenue?

### Common traits

Small number of engineers
Limited analytics infrastructure
Many guesses, few controlled experiments
Growth ideas come from founders, marketing, product, or sales
Success is measured loosely

The main goal is learning what moves the business.

## 2. Instrumentation and measurement

The team realizes that growth cannot scale without trustworthy data. Engineering work shifts toward event tracking, funnels, attribution, dashboards, data quality, and experiment readiness.

The team is usually asking

Can we trust the numbers?

### Common traits

Clearer funnel definitions
Analytics events become standardized
Conversion metrics are agreed upon
Teams start separating acquisition, activation, retention, and monetization
Experiment results become easier to interpret

The main goal is making growth measurable.

## 3. Experimentation engine

Once measurement improves, the team starts running structured experiments. Growth becomes less opinion driven and more hypothesis driven.

The team is usually asking

How many quality experiments can we run, and how fast can we learn?

### Common traits

Experiment backlog
Clear hypotheses
A and B testing systems
Feature flags
Defined success metrics
Post experiment reviews
More collaboration between engineering, product, design, marketing, and data science

The main goal is increasing learning velocity.

## 4. Funnel specialization

As the company grows, the growth team often splits focus across parts of the customer journey.

Typical areas include

Acquisition
Activation
Onboarding
Engagement
Retention
Referral
Expansion
Revenue conversion

The team is usually asking

Which part of the funnel has the biggest constraint right now?

### Common traits

Dedicated pods or subteams
Deeper ownership of specific metrics
More sophisticated user segmentation
More personalization
Closer connection to lifecycle marketing and product analytics

The main goal is improving the highest leverage bottlenecks.

## 5. Platform and systems growth

At this stage, the team stops relying only on individual experiments and starts building reusable growth infrastructure.

The team is usually asking

What systems can make every future experiment faster or more effective?

### Common traits

Experimentation platforms
Personalization engines
Notification systems
Pricing and packaging infrastructure
Referral systems
Lifecycle messaging platforms
Growth data pipelines
Reusable onboarding components

The main goal is turning growth into a scalable internal platform.

## 6. Predictable growth model

The team now understands which levers reliably drive growth. The work becomes more strategic and tied to financial planning.

The team is usually asking

How do we predictably compound growth over quarters and years?

### Common traits

Clear growth model
Reliable leading indicators
Forecastable acquisition and retention loops
Strong link between product usage and revenue
Better prioritization across channels and product surfaces
Growth engineering influences company strategy

The main goal is making growth repeatable and forecastable.

## 7. Mature growth organization

In mature companies, growth engineering becomes both centralized and embedded. Some engineers work on shared platforms, while others sit inside product areas to drive growth outcomes.

The team is usually asking

How do we keep finding meaningful growth without damaging user trust or product quality?

### Common traits

Strong experimentation governance
High bar for user experience
Guardrails for privacy, deliverability, performance, and brand trust
More incremental gains
More emphasis on long term retention and monetization
Growth principles spread across the whole product organization

The main goal is sustainable growth, not just short term conversion lifts.

## The simple version

Growth engineering usually evolves from

Shipping quick ideas
Measuring the funnel
Running experiments
Specializing by funnel area
Building reusable platforms
Creating a predictable growth model
Operating as a mature growth organization

The biggest shift is that early growth teams chase wins, while mature growth teams build systems that repeatedly produce wins.
